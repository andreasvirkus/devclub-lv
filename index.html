Klaus 2017

The spark ‚ú®

So, it's the blissful year of 2017. Let's start with our founders. Martin was the global head of customer support of Pipedrive at the time, trying to figure out the most optimal way of tracking and measuring the quality of his team's support interactions. Excel quickly became painful, taking minutes to even load up the spreadsheet itself. Kair, Martin's lifelong friend and partner in crime for many former endeavours, decides to find a "less talk, let's build" developer (Egon - founder #3) to construct the first prototype of Klaus. After the initial pitch to Egon, they started meeting weekly and exchanged ideas/visions for the product. Egon worked on the prototype in the evenings and iterated as quickly as possible on the small feedback sessions. Your first prototype should be like applying for a job - you'll need to show up, be dressed properly and leave a spectacular impression. You'll obviously need to perform once you're hired (aka land a client), but that **comes later**. Sell the best version of yourself (how you vision the product to work), but also be honest of where you're at today. For example, the prototype of Klaus was a Laravel app with great UX, but not the greatest architectural design to later scale the product. It'd hit the client's API rate limits, but it **sold the vision of Klaus**. And that's all that matters - as all those issues are already a **good problem to have**. As developers, we often strive for the most universal & elegant design of our systems, one that would withheld all of Facebook's current users migrating over to your small notes app. But that's a mindset you need to break when bootstrapping a startup. You need to acknowledge that your solutions will be dirty. It'll be hack-on-a-hack-on-a-hack for a while, until you can **afford to** take a step back - refactor & redesign things.

Klaus 2018: part I

Gone fishing üê≥

Our MVP launches, Klaus lands their first two clients and pre-seed funding. Time to grow! Your first clients are crucial. Talk to them frequently, keep them in the loop about developments and features planned and they become like your team members. You get a very short feedback loop around everything + those bugs they discover will be reported with a lot less frustration if they feel heard. It's tricky to profile early employees - you'll need passionate multitools, who can adapt to anything. But you can't throw an interviewee into a burning room and say > _"Let's see you adapt to this one. Bonus points if you're passionate about it and also man the customer support at the same time."_ You might even find it hard to anyone devote their time and risk it to a startup in that early of a phase. In general, you're looking for special ops, not privates.

Klaus 2018: part II

The build-up üî®

Summer: two hothead developer-cowboys join and declare they want to build a new thing from scratch. => Discuss through the architectural trade-offs, etc It's very stereotypical for a new developer to fly in and state that the old must go and they can always do it better. So look for rational arguments and hear them out, but remain sceptical. Fall: we almost freeze the product, working full-time on the new version from scratch. Egon continues to deliver features on the MVP, but some features are cut to not deliver them twice (both in the MVP that's still live + v1.0). Meanwhile sales does a lot of cold outreach, teasing the promise of v1.0, as the new infrastructure enables a lot of things that both the current prototype doesn't and it's often things that the users are looking for. Scope creep is also tricky to manage, as each new lead has a thing they **definitely need** to even consider you. If you're a team that can iterate fast, those **must-have** things can be a great way to impress some leads, especially if you can translate them into a little more universal features that can benefit everyone. We get the v1.0 launched within 2 months. It feels as a huge accomplishment, but has little impact on the business. Meanwhile, our runway's stretching mighty thin. The year - despite all the 8-23 work days - ends on a bitter note.

Notes from the field

People. Love. Swag. If you have a great product and a kick-ass team, it won't matter if you've existed for 20 years or just one (or if your team has 100 members or 10). We were able to close medium & large size clients out the gate and being small & nimble often benefitted us - as we could go the extra mile where our competitors often couldn't. As a startup, you're usually out to disrupt something (at least I hope you are). You'll either change something existing or pave the way into new territory. As Klaus is shaping a new product category, you also need to be mindful to shape the world around it. Often, instead of a sales meeting, you'll end up simply educating your potential users about the new field (and just growing interest in your product in general). We've managed to coin new terms (e.g. conversation review, IQS) that now get used by our partners (Intercom)
